Forge your own: why everyone should own their language models

Today Smedjan is public — a pure-Rust engine that trains and runs language models from scratch, with no Python, no PyTorch, and no cloud. Here is why I built it and what it is for.

The ability to build a language model has concentrated into a handful of frameworks and a handful of clouds. That is efficient, and it is also a kind of dependence: your model lives in someone else's stack, behind someone else's API, subject to someone else's terms. For many uses that is fine. For the ones that matter most to me — a private agent on my own machine, an internal tool for a team, something that keeps working when the network does not — it is not.

Smedjan is a bet that ownership should be possible for one person. The whole pipeline is one Rust binary: train a tokenizer, prepare data, pretrain, distill, fine-tune, align, quantize, export, and serve — Metal on Apple Silicon, CUDA on NVIDIA, the same checkpoint format across both. The dependency tree is four small crates plus the GPU bindings. You can read every line.

The headline on the home page is written, live, by a 295K-parameter model compiled to WebAssembly and running in your browser — the engine demonstrating itself end to end, with no server. It is small on purpose: the point is not scale, it is that the whole loop is yours.

It is early, and honest about it: safetensors import is F32-only today, some mixers are experimental, and faithful HF inference parity is on the roadmap. If that excites you rather than worries you, come help or back the work.

— Andrei
